This case study outlines how I led a multi‑year modernization of a legacy search platform that powered a large enterprise product ecosystem. The initiative focused on improving relevance quality, scalability, and readiness for AI‑driven capabilities across 250+ product databases and 15 SaaS applications.
## Problem Statement 
Our search stack was proprietary and originally built for lexical research, which has become less common and increasingly misaligned with how users expect to search today. This created several issues that were becoming hard to ignore:

The experience produced high user friction and was contributing to declining usage, because customers wanted more natural, intuitive ways to search that our system wasn’t designed to support.

Only a small number of employees still understood the inner workings of the engine, which created long‑term sustainability and operational risk.

There was clear pressure from the market and from leadership to modernize our search experience and support more contemporary approaches to query understanding.

The system itself had technical limitations that made it difficult to scale, evolve, or meet new product expectations.

In short, the legacy search engine had become a blocker for users, for product teams, and for the company’s ability to keep pace with the market.
## Strategy
My strategy was to start small, prove value early, and build the foundations for a scalable, modern search architecture. Because the legacy system was too large and too specialized for a full rewrite, the focus was on creating a flexible structure, shifting how teams approached the development lifecycle, and bringing people along with empathy and clarity.

The strategy centered on a few core principles:

* Start with a small, well‑defined slice of the problem. Validate the new architecture and orchestration model before expanding to the broader ecosystem.

* Establish a scalable architecture early. Introduce clearer service boundaries and a more flexible content storage model so future capabilities could be added without constant rework.

* Shift the organization’s mental model around the SDLC. AI‑related work doesn’t follow a traditional “requirements → build → test → release” pattern. It requires evaluation loops, iterative tuning, and a different approach to quality. Helping teams understand this was essential to making the modernization sustainable.

* Use agentic orchestration to simplify complexity. Replace hard‑coded logic with a more dynamic orchestration layer that coordinates retrieval, processing, and ranking in a predictable, maintainable way.

* Lead with empathy. Modernization can feel disruptive, especially for teams deeply invested in the legacy system. I focused on listening, explaining the “why,” and making sure the new model felt accessible rather than threatening.

* Let modern capabilities guide the roadmap. As the architecture matured, use improved query handling, relevance workflows, and content processing to determine what we built next — not the constraints of the old system.

* Build toward a platform‑level solution. Create a shared foundation multiple teams could adopt, reducing duplication and improving consistency across the organizatio
## Execution 
Execution focused on proving out the new approach in a practical, low‑risk way while helping teams adjust to a different development lifecycle. A big part of the work was making sure people had the support, context, and confidence they needed to build in a new paradigm.

Key parts of the execution included:

* We invested in learning early. As a group, we agreed the team needed exposure to how other organizations were approaching similar systems, so we sent developers to a conference that covered those patterns. It helped us align on terminology, workflows, and expectations before diving into the modernization work.

* Defining scope with the business early. I worked quickly with stakeholders to clarify what we were solving, what success looked like, and what constraints we needed to respect. This alignment made it possible to move fast without creating confusion downstream.

* Delivering a productionized MVP. We built a small, contained version of the new architecture and orchestration model and shipped it into production. This gave us real usage data, validated the approach, and demonstrated that the modernization was achievable.

* Running pre‑production customer betas. We invited select customers to test early versions of the new experience. Their feedback helped refine the workflows and gave us confidence that the new model was solving real problems.

* Working as a group — not in silos. The modernization wasn’t something I pushed onto teams; it was something we built together. We collaborated closely across engineering, architecture, and product, making decisions as a group and sharing ownership of the outcomes.

* Breaking the work into incremental milestones. Instead of trying to modernize everything at once, I sequenced the work into clear, achievable steps. Each milestone delivered value, reduced risk, and helped teams adjust to the new development lifecycle.

* Securing funding along the way. I didn’t ask for a massive upfront investment. Instead, I requested funding in stages, tied to milestone outcomes. This made the effort easier to support and kept leadership engaged in the progress.

* Overall, execution was about learning together, proving value early, and guiding the organization through a new way of building search‑related capabilities.
